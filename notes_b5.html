R78 · Alternative Investment Features, Methods, and Structures
Reading 78 — Alternative Investments
Asset Classes & Characteristics
Investment Structures
Fund-of-Funds & Key Risks
  • Hedge Funds: pooled vehicles using leverage, short selling, derivatives; absolute return focus
  • Private Equity: buyouts, venture capital, growth equity; illiquid, long horizon
  • Real Estate: direct ownership or REITs; income + appreciation
  • Commodities: physical goods; inflation hedge; spot vs. futures exposure
  • Digital Assets: cryptocurrencies, tokens; high volatility, regulatory uncertainty
  • Infrastructure: long-lived physical assets; stable cash flows, inflation-linked
  • Low correlation to traditional assets → diversification benefit
  • Higher return potential but also higher risk, lower liquidity
  • Limited Partnership (LP): most common PE/hedge fund structure
  • GP manages the fund; has unlimited liability
  • LPs provide capital; liability limited to investment
  • Closed-end fund: fixed term, capital called/distributed over life
  • Open-end fund: investors can subscribe/redeem (most hedge funds)
  • Management fee: 1–2% of AUM annually
  • Incentive fee (carry): 20% of profits above hurdle rate
  • Hurdle rate: minimum return before carry is earned
  • Clawback: GP returns excess carry if later losses occur
  • High-water mark: must recover previous losses before new carry
  • Fund-of-Funds (FoF): invests in multiple alt funds; adds layer of diversification
  • Double fee layer: investor pays FoF fees + underlying fund fees → performance drag
  • Access benefit: access to top managers otherwise closed
  • Liquidity risk: lock-up periods, redemption gates, side pockets
  • Leverage risk: amplifies gains AND losses
  • Valuation risk: illiquid assets hard to price; stale pricing
  • Transparency risk: limited disclosure to investors
  • Operational risk: fraud, back-office failures
  • Counterparty risk: OTC derivatives exposure
R79 · Alternative Investment Performance and Returns
Reading 79 — Alternative Investments
IRR & MOIC / TVPI
J-Curve & Cash Flow Timing
Benchmarking & Biases
  • IRR (Internal Rate of Return): time-weighted return for PE; accounts for timing of cash flows
  • NPV = 0 → solve for IRR
  • MOIC (Multiple on Invested Capital): total value / invested capital; ignores time
  • MOIC = (Realized + Unrealized) / Capital Invested
  • TVPI (Total Value to Paid-In): same as MOIC conceptually; = DPI + RVPI
  • DPI: Distributed to Paid-In (realized gains only)
  • RVPI: Residual Value to Paid-In (unrealized NAV)
  • J-Curve effect: early years show negative returns (fees + unrealized losses); positive returns emerge later as investments mature
  • Capital called early (deployment) → distributions come years later
  • Vintage year: year fund makes first investment; used to compare funds
PhaseCash FlowTypical Year
InvestmentNegative (calls)1–4
Value creationNear zero3–6
HarvestingPositive (distributions)5–10+
  • Survivorship bias: failed funds not reported → overstated returns in databases
  • Backfill bias (instant history bias): funds add historical data when joining database → upward bias
  • Stale pricing: infrequent valuation smooths returns → understates volatility
  • Benchmarking challenges: no single agreed benchmark; peer group comparisons common
  • Public market equivalent (PME): compares PE returns to equivalent public index investment
  • Correlations may appear low due to smoothing, not true diversification
R80 · Investments in Private Capital: Equity and Debt
Reading 80 — Alternative Investments
Venture Capital Stages
Leveraged Buyouts (LBO)
Private Debt & Exit Strategies
  • Seed stage: concept/idea; highest risk; small capital amounts
  • Early stage (Series A/B): product development, initial revenues; still pre-profitability
  • Late stage (growth/expansion): scaling operations; proven business model
  • Pre-IPO / bridge: final round before public listing
  • VC investors receive preferred stock (liquidation preference, anti-dilution rights)
  • Post-money valuation = pre-money + new investment
  • VC targets high IRR (25–35%+) to compensate for high failure rates
  • LBO: acquisition financed mostly with debt; target's cash flows service debt
  • Typical capital structure: 60–70% debt, 30–40% equity
  • Value creation levers: (1) operational improvements, (2) multiple expansion, (3) leverage paydown
  • Senior secured debt: lowest cost, first priority
  • Mezzanine debt: subordinated; higher yield; may include warrants or equity kickers
  • PIK (payment-in-kind): interest paid in additional debt, not cash
  • Equity holders (GP) get residual value after debt repaid
  • PE exit strategies:
  • IPO: highest valuation potential; subject to market conditions; lock-up period applies
  • Strategic sale (M&A): sale to corporate acquirer; synergy premium possible
  • Secondary sale: sale to another PE fund; faster but lower premium
  • Recapitalization: additional debt raised; distribute proceeds to equity holders
  • Liquidation: wind-down; typically worst outcome
  • Private debt: direct lending, distressed debt, venture debt, mezzanine
  • Offers illiquidity premium over public bonds
R81 · Real Estate and Infrastructure
Reading 81 — Alternative Investments
Real Estate Investment Forms
Key RE Valuation Metrics
Infrastructure Characteristics
  • Direct investment: ownership of physical property; high capital, illiquid, active management
  • REITs (Real Estate Investment Trusts): publicly traded; liquid; must distribute ≥90% of taxable income
  • Private RE funds: LP structure; closed-end; leveraged
  • CMBS (Commercial MBS): debt exposure to commercial RE; more liquid
  • Mortgage REITs: hold mortgages, not properties
  • RE provides income + capital appreciation + inflation hedge
  • Disadvantages: illiquidity, management burden, transaction costs
  • NOI (Net Operating Income): rental revenue − operating expenses (excl. financing & taxes)
  • NOI = Gross Revenue − Vacancy − Expenses
  • Cap Rate (Capitalization Rate):
  • Cap Rate = NOI / Property Value
  • Value = NOI / Cap Rate
  • Lower cap rate → higher valuation (prime properties)
  • NAV (Net Asset Value): market value of assets − liabilities; used for private RE funds
  • FFO (Funds from Operations): REIT earnings + D&A − gains on sales; preferred performance metric
  • FFO = Net Income + D&A − Property Gains
  • Infrastructure: toll roads, airports, utilities, pipelines, hospitals, schools
  • Characteristics: long asset life, high capital intensity, monopoly-like, regulated revenues
  • Greenfield: new construction; higher risk, higher return potential
  • Brownfield: existing assets; lower risk, stable cash flows
  • Infrastructure cash flows are bond-like: stable, long-duration, often inflation-linked
  • Good for liability-driven investors (pension funds, insurance)
  • Access via direct investment, listed infrastructure funds, or unlisted funds
  • Risks: regulatory/political risk, construction risk (greenfield), usage/demand risk
R82 · Natural Resources
Reading 82 — Alternative Investments
Commodity Price Drivers
Futures Curve: Contango & Backwardation
Timberland, Farmland & Features
  • Supply factors: production capacity, reserves, extraction costs, geopolitics, weather
  • Demand factors: economic growth, industrial activity, substitution, technology
  • Storage costs: physical commodities incur storage, insurance; affect pricing
  • Commodities are real assets → strong inflation hedge
  • No cash flow yield; return from price appreciation + roll yield + collateral yield
  • Spot price: current market price for immediate delivery
  • Futures price: agreed price for future delivery
  • Futures = Spot × e^[(r + storage − convenience yield) × t]
  • Contango: futures price > spot price; normal for storable commodities with carrying costs
  • Rolling long futures position in contango → negative roll yield (sell low, buy high)
  • Backwardation: futures price < spot price; occurs when convenience yield is high or spot demand elevated
  • Rolling long futures in backwardation → positive roll yield (sell high, buy low)
Curve ShapeRoll YieldDriver
ContangoNegativeStorage costs dominate
BackwardationPositiveConvenience yield dominates
  • Timberland: biological growth adds value independent of prices; diversification from harvesting timing flexibility
  • Returns: land appreciation + timber price changes + biological growth
  • Farmland: income from crop/livestock production + land appreciation
  • Both have low correlation with stocks/bonds; inflation hedge
  • Commodity exposure via: spot (physical), futures, ETFs, commodity producer stocks
  • Producer stocks add equity market beta; not pure commodity play
  • Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs): midstream energy infrastructure; yield-focused
R83 · Hedge Funds
Reading 83 — Alternative Investments
Key Strategies
Fees, Leverage & Liquidity
Strategy Risk Profiles
  • Long/Short Equity: long undervalued + short overvalued; variable net exposure; most common strategy
  • Global Macro: top-down bets on currencies, rates, commodities; high leverage; directional
  • Relative Value / Arbitrage: exploit price discrepancies (convertible arb, fixed income arb, statistical arb)
  • Event-Driven: merger arb, distressed securities, activist investing; catalyst-dependent
  • Managed Futures (CTA): trend-following in futures markets; systematic; good in crises
  • Equity Market Neutral: zero net exposure; pure alpha focus
  • 2-and-20 fee structure: 2% management fee on AUM + 20% incentive fee on profits
  • Actual industry average trending lower (1.4% mgmt / 17% incentive)
  • High-water mark: incentive fee only on new profits above prior peak NAV
  • Hurdle rate: minimum return (e.g., LIBOR/SOFR) before incentive fee applies
  • Leverage: use of borrowing, derivatives; amplifies both returns and losses
  • Lock-up period: capital cannot be withdrawn (typically 1–2 years)
  • Redemption gates: fund limits withdrawal amounts during stress
  • Side pockets: illiquid assets segregated; investors cannot redeem
  • Prime broker provides leverage, stock lending, and custody services

R84 · Introduction to Digital Assets
Reading 84 — Alternative Investments
Blockchain & Cryptocurrency
DeFi & Tokenization
Risks & Regulatory Environment
  • Blockchain: distributed, immutable ledger; records transactions without central authority
  • Proof of Work (PoW): miners solve cryptographic puzzles; energy intensive (Bitcoin)
  • Proof of Stake (PoS): validators stake coins; more energy efficient (Ethereum post-merge)
  • Cryptocurrency: digital currency secured by cryptography; no central bank backing
  • Bitcoin: store of value/"digital gold"; fixed supply (21M); most liquid
  • Altcoins: Ethereum, Solana, etc.; platform functionality beyond payments
  • Stablecoins: pegged to fiat (USDT, USDC); reduce volatility but carry counterparty risk
  • DeFi (Decentralized Finance): financial services on blockchain without intermediaries; smart contracts execute automatically
  • Smart contracts: self-executing code on blockchain; enable lending, trading, insurance
  • NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens): unique digital assets representing ownership of art, collectibles, etc.
  • Tokenization: representing real-world assets (RE, art, bonds) as blockchain tokens; enables fractional ownership
  • CBDCs (Central Bank Digital Currencies): government-issued digital fiat; different from crypto
  • Potential: 24/7 markets, faster settlement, financial inclusion
  • Volatility risk: extreme price swings; no fundamental anchor for many coins
  • Regulatory risk: evolving global rules; potential bans or restrictions
  • Custody/security risk: exchange hacks, lost private keys → irreversible loss
  • Liquidity risk: many tokens thinly traded; wide spreads
  • Fraud/scam risk: rug pulls, Ponzi schemes, wash trading
  • Technology risk: smart contract bugs, protocol failures
  • ESG concerns: PoW mining energy consumption
  • Regulatory approaches vary: US (SEC/CFTC jurisdiction debates), EU (MiCA framework), Asia (varied)
  • Portfolio allocation: typically small (<5%) given volatility
R85 · Portfolio Management Overview
Reading 85 — Portfolio Management
Portfolio Perspective
Types of Investors
Portfolio Management Process
  • Portfolio perspective: evaluate each investment in context of the whole portfolio, not in isolation
  • Diversification reduces unsystematic (specific) risk; cannot eliminate systematic (market) risk
  • Key insight: combining assets with low correlation reduces total portfolio risk below weighted average of individual risks
  • σ²(port) = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂σ₁σ₂ρ₁₂
  • As ρ decreases → portfolio variance decreases → diversification benefit increases
  • Individual investors: unique goals, time horizon, tax situation; behavioral biases significant
  • Pension funds: DB (liability-driven, long horizon) vs DC (member bears risk)
  • Endowments/foundations: perpetual horizon; spending rule (e.g., 5% annually); total return focus
  • Insurance companies: match assets to liabilities; regulatory constraints; conservative
  • Sovereign Wealth Funds: government-owned; long horizon; varied mandates
  • Banks: manage balance sheet; liquidity crucial; regulatory capital requirements
  • Step 1 — Planning: understand client, prepare IPS (Investment Policy Statement)
  • Step 2 — Execution: asset allocation, security selection, portfolio construction
  • Step 3 — Feedback: monitor, rebalance, measure and report performance
  • IPS is the cornerstone document; defines objectives and constraints
  • Asset managers: buy-side (portfolio managers) vs sell-side (research/sales)
  • Active management: seeks alpha vs benchmark; higher fees
  • Passive management: replicates index; lower fees, lower turnover
R86 · Basics of Portfolio Planning and Construction
Reading 86 — Portfolio Management
IPS Components
RRTTLLU Constraints
Strategic Asset Allocation
  • IPS (Investment Policy Statement): formal document governing the investment relationship
  • IPS components:
    • Client description and background
    • Investment objectives (return + risk)
    • Investment constraints
    • Investment guidelines (permitted assets, restrictions)
    • Performance evaluation criteria
    • Procedures for review and modification
  • Return objective: may be stated as absolute (e.g., 7% p.a.) or relative (beat benchmark by 200 bps)
  • RRTTLLU mnemonic for IPS constraints:
  • Risk — willingness AND ability to take risk (both must be considered)
  • Return — target return requirement
  • Time Horizon — investment period length; longer → more risk tolerance
  • Tax — taxable vs tax-exempt accounts; affects asset location
  • Liquidity — need for cash; reduces risky/illiquid allocations
  • Legal / Regulatory — ERISA, trust law, regulatory limits
  • Unique Circumstances — ESG preferences, concentrated positions, restrictions
  • Strategic Asset Allocation (SAA): long-term target weights across asset classes; driven by IPS objectives
  • Based on capital market assumptions: expected returns, volatilities, correlations
  • Tactical Asset Allocation (TAA): short-term deviations from SAA to exploit market opportunities
  • Efficient frontier: set of portfolios with highest return for given risk
  • Risk-free asset + risky portfolio → Capital Allocation Line (CAL)
  • Optimal portfolio: tangency point of highest Sharpe ratio portfolio on efficient frontier
  • Rebalancing: periodic or threshold-based; maintains target allocation
R87 · Behavioral Biases of Individuals
Reading 87 — Portfolio Management
Cognitive Errors: Belief Perseverance
Cognitive Errors: Information Processing
Emotional Biases & Portfolio Implications
  • Cognitive errors stem from faulty reasoning; more correctable with education
  • Conservatism: slow to update beliefs with new information; under-react to new data
  • Confirmation bias: seek/interpret info that confirms existing views; ignore contradictory data
  • Representativeness: classify based on superficial similarity; ignore base rates (gambler's fallacy)
  • Illusion of control: believe they can control outcomes that are random
  • Hindsight bias: "I knew it all along"; overestimate ability to predict
  • Anchoring & adjustment: over-rely on initial information (anchor); insufficient adjustment
  • Mental accounting: treat money differently based on source/purpose; inconsistent with holistic portfolio view
  • Framing: decisions affected by how information is presented (gain vs loss frame)
  • Availability heuristic: overweight recent/vivid events; overestimate probability of memorable outcomes
  • Conjunction fallacy: think joint event more probable than single event
  • Emotional biases stem from feelings; harder to correct; must accommodate
  • Loss aversion: losses hurt ~2× more than equal gains feel good; hold losers too long
  • Overconfidence: over-precise estimates; excessive trading; under-diversification
  • Self-control: prefer immediate gratification; under-save for retirement
  • Status quo bias: preference for current state; resist changes to portfolio
  • Endowment effect: overvalue assets already owned
  • Regret aversion: avoid decisions that may lead to regret; herding behavior
  • Portfolio implication: underdiversification, excessive trading, suboptimal allocation
R88 · Introduction to Risk Management
Reading 88 — Portfolio Management
Risk Governance & ERM
Types of Financial & Non-Financial Risk
Measuring Risk: VaR, CVaR & Risk Budgeting
  • Risk governance: board-level oversight of firm-wide risk; sets risk appetite and policies
  • Enterprise Risk Management (ERM): integrated framework covering all organizational risks; holistic view
  • Risk budget: allocation of acceptable risk across portfolio/strategies; ensures total risk ≤ capacity
  • Risk management process:
    • Identify risks
    • Measure/quantify risks
    • Manage/mitigate risks
    • Monitor and report
  • Risk tolerance ≠ risk capacity; must consider both
  • Financial risks:
    • Market risk: adverse price movements (equity, FX, rates, commodities)
    • Credit risk: counterparty default or credit quality deterioration
    • Liquidity risk: inability to transact at fair value; funding liquidity vs market liquidity
  • Non-financial risks:
    • Operational risk: people, processes, systems, external events
    • Model risk: incorrect model assumptions or implementation errors
    • Settlement risk: one party pays but counterparty fails to deliver
    • Regulatory/legal risk: changes in laws or compliance failures
    • Reputational risk: damage to firm's brand/trust
    • Tail risk: extreme events in distributional tails
  • VaR (Value at Risk): maximum expected loss at given confidence level over specified period
  • Example: "1-day 95% VaR = $1M" → 95% confident loss will not exceed $1M in one day
  • Limitations: says nothing about losses BEYOND VaR; assumes normal distributions; not sub-additive
  • CVaR (Conditional VaR / Expected Shortfall): expected loss given loss exceeds VaR; better tail risk measure
  • CVaR = E[Loss | Loss > VaR]
  • Stress testing: evaluate portfolio under extreme but plausible scenarios
  • Scenario analysis: historical (1987 crash, 2008) or hypothetical scenarios
  • Risk budgeting: allocate a risk limit (VaR, volatility, tracking error) to each PM/strategy
R89 · Ethics and Trust in the Investment Profession
Reading 89 — Ethical and Professional Standards
Ethics Framework & Challenges
Ethical Decision-Making Model
Why Ethics Matters in Finance
  • Ethics: system of moral principles that guide behavior; standards for evaluating actions as right or wrong
  • Ethical principles go beyond legal compliance; law is minimum standard
  • Challenges to ethical behavior:
    • Self-interest: personal gain can override professional duty
    • Situational influences: social pressure, authority, environment
    • Short-term thinking: focus on immediate outcomes
    • Overconfidence: belief that own judgment is always correct
  • Good character + ethical education → better outcomes
  • CFA Institute ethical decision-making framework:
  • Step 1: Identify relevant facts and stakeholders
  • Step 2: Identify relevant ethical principles and conflicts
  • Step 3: Consider situational influences (pressures, incentives)
  • Step 4: Decide and act; choose course of action consistent with ethical principles
  • Step 5: Reflect and learn from the experience
  • "Mirror test": would you be comfortable if actions were reported in newspaper?
  • Ask: "Is it legal? Is it fair? Is it right?"
  • Finance operates on trust; markets rely on confidence in integrity
  • Investment professionals manage other people's money → fiduciary duty
  • Ethical behavior supports:
    • Client trust and long-term relationships
    • Market efficiency and integrity
    • Stability of financial system
    • Profession's reputation
  • Unethical behavior destroys trust → undermines market function
  • Regulatory compliance is floor; CFA standards aim ABOVE the floor
  • Profession vs occupation: professions demand higher ethical standards and client-first orientation

Reading 90 — Ethical and Professional Standards
6 Components of the Code of Ethics
Overview of the 7 Standards
Key Obligations & Priority Rules
  • CFA Members and Candidates must:
  • 1. Act with integrity, competence, diligence, respect, and in an ethical manner with the public, clients, prospective clients, employers, employees, colleagues, and regulators
  • 2. Place the integrity of the investment profession and interests of clients above personal interests
  • 3. Use reasonable care and exercise independent professional judgment
  • 4. Practice and encourage others to practice in a professional and ethical manner
  • 5. Promote the integrity and viability of the global capital markets
  • 6. Maintain and improve professional competence and strive to maintain the competence of other investment professionals
  • Standard I: Professionalism
  • Standard II: Integrity of Capital Markets
  • Standard III: Duties to Clients
  • Standard IV: Duties to Employers
  • Standard V: Investment Analysis, Recommendations, and Actions
  • Standard VI: Conflicts of Interest
  • Standard VII: Responsibilities as a CFA Institute Member or CFA Candidate
  • Standards apply to all CFA members and candidates globally regardless of local law
  • Where local law conflicts: follow stricter of the two requirements
  • Priority of transactions: Clients first → Employer second → Member's own interests last
  • Fiduciary duty: act in best interest of clients at all times
  • Independence and objectivity must be maintained; gifts/entertainment that could compromise independence must be declined
  • Prohibition on material nonpublic information (MNPI) trading → market integrity
  • Standards apply whether or not there is a specific law prohibiting the conduct
  • Best execution: seek best price/terms for client transactions
R91 · Guidance for Standards I–VII
Reading 91 — Ethical and Professional Standards
Standard I: Professionalism
Standard II: Capital Market Integrity
  • I(A) Knowledge of the Law: understand and comply with all applicable laws; must NOT participate in or assist violations; if conflict, follow stricter standard; disassociate from illegal activity
  • I(B) Independence and Objectivity: maintain free from outside pressures; do not accept gifts/compensation that could compromise independence; modest gifts OK; research must be objective
  • I(C) Misrepresentation: do not make false or misleading statements about qualifications, services, performance; do not plagiarize; cite sources; model/methodology misrepresentation prohibited
  • I(D) Misconduct: no acts involving dishonesty, fraud, deceit; personal conduct reflecting adversely on profession
  • II(A) Material Nonpublic Information (MNPI): do not trade or cause others to trade on MNPI; information is material if a reasonable investor would use it in investment decision; nonpublic if not generally disseminated
  • Mosaic theory: analyst may combine non-material nonpublic info + public info to reach conclusion → this is permitted
  • Firewall: information barriers between investment banking and research/trading; must be maintained
  • II(B) Market Manipulation: do not engage in practices that distort prices or artificially inflate volume; includes spreading false information or using transactions to mislead others about supply/demand
Standard III: Duties to Clients
Standard IV: Duties to Employers
  • III(A) Loyalty, Prudence & Care: fiduciary duty; client interests above personal; use judgment a prudent person would use; soft dollar commissions must benefit clients
  • III(B) Fair Dealing: treat all clients fairly when disseminating recommendations or taking investment actions; do NOT discriminate; system for equitable distribution required
  • III(C) Suitability: know your client (KYC); assess risk tolerance, time horizon, objectives; unsuitable recommendations violate this standard; IPS must be updated regularly
  • III(D) Performance Presentation: do not misrepresent performance; include relevant benchmarks; composite returns; don't cherry-pick
  • III(E) Preservation of Confidentiality: maintain confidentiality of client info; exceptions: illegal activity, required by law, client consent
  • IV(A) Loyalty to Employer: act in employer's best interest; do NOT take client lists, proprietary info when leaving; planning to leave is NOT a violation; must give notice
  • IV(B) Additional Compensation: must obtain written permission from employer before accepting compensation from outside sources that may create conflicts of interest
  • IV(C) Responsibilities of Supervisors: must make reasonable efforts to detect and prevent violations by subordinates; implement compliance procedures; delegate supervisory authority properly
  • Whistleblowing: if employer engages in illegal activity, may report to authorities; not required but permitted
Standard V: Investment Analysis & Actions
Standards VI & VII: Conflicts & CFA Responsibilities
  • V(A) Diligence and Reasonable Basis: exercise diligence, independence, thoroughness; reasonable and adequate basis for recommendations; when using third-party research, assess quality
  • V(B) Communication with Clients: distinguish fact from opinion; disclose limitations of analysis; use reasonable judgment; communicate material changes in process
  • V(C) Record Retention: maintain records supporting analysis and recommendations; minimum 7 years recommended; electronic records acceptable; member responsible for retention
  • VI(A) Disclosure of Conflicts: proactively disclose all conflicts of interest to clients and employer; includes ownership of covered securities, board memberships, compensation arrangements
  • VI(B) Priority of Transactions: client transactions > employer proprietary > member personal; no front-running client orders; personal trading policies (60-day holding period suggested)
  • VI(C) Referral Fees: disclose any fees received or paid for referring clients; disclosure must be made before engagement so client can assess bias
  • VII(A) Conduct as Member: do not engage in conduct that compromises integrity of CFA exams; do not misrepresent exam content
  • VII(B) Reference to CFA: do not exaggerate CFA significance; cannot say "CFA Level I passed" implies superiority; use correct references (e.g., "CFA charterholder", not "CFA analyst")
R92 · Introduction to GIPS
Reading 92 — Ethical and Professional Standards
Purpose & Compliance
Composites & Key Requirements
Verification & Benefits
  • GIPS (Global Investment Performance Standards): voluntary ethical standards for investment firms to calculate and present performance fairly and consistently
  • Developed by CFA Institute; globally recognized
  • Who must comply: the investment management firm claims compliance, not individuals
  • Compliance is firm-wide: cannot claim compliance for a single product
  • Applies to prospective clients; does not apply retroactively to non-GIPS periods
  • Purpose: create global standard for transparent, comparable performance presentation; build investor trust
  • Composite: aggregation of portfolios with similar investment strategy/objective; must include ALL actual fee-paying, discretionary portfolios in at least one composite
  • Cannot exclude poor-performing accounts; cannot cherry-pick
  • New portfolios added after 1 full month under management
  • Terminated portfolios must be included for periods they were managed
  • Key requirements:
    • Returns calculated after trading expenses (gross or net disclosed)
    • Composite returns asset-weighted
    • Minimum 5-year track record (build to 10 years)
    • External cash flows must be handled consistently (TWRR)
    • Disclosures: composite definition, number of portfolios, internal dispersion, benchmark
  • Verification: independent third-party review of firm-wide GIPS compliance; NOT required but recommended
  • Verification covers whether firm has complied with GIPS requirements firm-wide; does NOT verify individual composite accuracy
  • Performance examination: deeper review of specific composite; separate from verification
  • Benefits of GIPS compliance:
    • Competitive advantage in marketing
    • Credibility with prospective clients globally
    • Standardizes internal performance measurement
    • Facilitates fair comparison across firms
  • GIPS does NOT guarantee future performance or accuracy of all data
R93 · Ethics Application
Reading 93 — Ethical and Professional Standards
MNPI & Research Scenarios
Client Duties & Conflicts
Common Exam Traps & Red Flags
  • Scenario: analyst receives tip from company CFO before earnings → must NOT trade; must report to compliance; even if tip inadvertently disclosed, it's MNPI
  • Mosaic theory application: analyst visits factories (public info), reads trade publications (public), observes increased truck activity → may form buy recommendation; PERMITTED
  • Research independence: investment banking pressure to issue favorable report → analyst must maintain independence (Std I(B)); document pressure and refusal
  • Plagiarism: using another firm's research without attribution → violates I(C); must cite sources
  • Social media: tweeting recommendations must meet same standards as formal reports
  • Suitability scenario: retired client asks to put 100% in tech stocks → unsuitable; must update IPS, discuss risk; cannot comply without discussion
  • Fair dealing scenario: hot IPO allocated only to top clients → violates III(B); must have equitable system; pro-rata or lottery acceptable
  • Soft dollars: using client commissions to buy research that benefits other clients or manager → must benefit the paying client; disclose to client
  • Leaving employer: taking client list without permission → violates IV(A); planning departure alone is OK; soliciting clients before leaving is NOT
  • Outside compensation: board seat at company covered by analyst → must disclose to employer AND clients (Std IV(B), VI(A))
  • Key exam traps:
  • Law vs Standards: if law is stricter → follow law; if CFA Standards are stricter → follow CFA Standards
  • Disclosure is NOT a cure-all: disclosing a conflict does NOT make the action acceptable if it still harms clients
  • Reasonable basis required EVEN IF client demands a specific recommendation
  • "Best price" for client may NOT always be the lowest commission (consider execution quality, services)
  • CFA designation: "CFA" must be used as adjective not noun; "John Smith, CFA" OK; "John Smith is a CFA" NOT OK
  • Red flags in exam questions: commission arrangements, gifts from issuers, analyst travel paid by company, personal trading in covered securities, family account treatment
  • When in doubt: disclose the conflict; place client interests first
