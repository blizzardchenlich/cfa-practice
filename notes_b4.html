Reading 54 · Fixed Income
Fixed-Income Bond Valuation: Prices and Yields
Bond Pricing (PV of Cash Flows)
Yield to Maturity (YTM)
Price-Yield Relationship
  • Bond price = PV of all future cash flows discounted at required yield
  • Semi-annual coupon bonds: divide coupon and rate by 2, double periods
  • Price > Par = premium bond (coupon > YTM)
  • Price < Par = discount bond (coupon < YTM)
  • Price = Par = par bond (coupon = YTM)
P = ∑ [C/(1+r)^t] + Par/(1+r)^N
  • YTM: IRR of bond's cash flows at current price
  • Assumes coupons reinvested at YTM — a key limitation
  • Annualized as BEY (bond equivalent yield) = 2 × semi-annual rate
  • YTM is the yield if held to maturity with no default
  • Find by trial and error / financial calculator
Solve for r: P = ∑[CF_t/(1+r)^t]
  • Inverse relationship: price falls when yield rises
  • Convex relationship — price rises more than it falls for equal yield changes
  • Pull-to-par: as maturity approaches, price converges to par
  • Longer maturity = more price sensitivity to yield changes
  • Lower coupon = more price sensitivity to yield changes

Yield and Yield Spread Measures for Fixed-Rate Bonds
Yield Measures
Spread Measures
OAS & Interpretation
  • Current yield = Annual coupon / Price
  • YTM: most widely used; IRR of cash flows
  • Yield to call (YTC): assumes bond called at first call date
  • Yield to worst (YTW): minimum of YTM, YTC, YTP
  • Street convention: assumes 30/360 day count
Current Yield = Annual Coupon / Price
  • G-spread: YTM − benchmark government bond yield
  • I-spread: YTM − swap rate (same maturity)
  • Z-spread (zero-volatility spread): constant spread added to spot curve to price bond
  • Z-spread > G-spread (reflects term structure shape)
  • All spreads measure compensation for credit/liquidity risk
  • OAS (option-adjusted spread): Z-spread minus option cost
  • OAS removes embedded option value — true credit/liquidity spread
  • For callable: Z-spread > OAS (call option has value to issuer)
  • For putable: Z-spread < OAS (put option has value to holder)
  • Higher OAS = more attractive (more compensation per unit risk)
OAS = Z-spread − Option Value

Yield and Yield Spread Measures for Floating-Rate Instruments
FRN Pricing
Quoted Margin vs Required Margin
Discount / Premium FRN
  • FRN: coupon resets each period based on reference rate + QM
  • If QM = required margin (RM): FRN priced at par on reset date
  • Price fluctuates between reset dates due to rate changes
  • Credit risk affects required margin; market rate affects reference
Coupon = Reference Rate + Quoted Margin
  • Quoted margin (QM): fixed spread stated in indenture
  • Required margin (RM): spread demanded by market now
  • RM increases if credit quality deteriorates
  • RM decreases if credit quality improves
  • Discount margin (DM): spread that makes PV of FRN cash flows = price
DM: solve P = ∑[(Ref+QM)/(1+Ref+DM)^t + Par/(1+Ref+DM)^N]
  • QM > RM → FRN trades at premium to par
  • QM < RM → FRN trades at discount to par
  • QM = RM → FRN trades at par
  • Interest rate risk of FRN is low (coupon resets)
  • Credit risk still present — if issuer quality changes, DM changes

Term Structure of Interest Rates
Spot, Par & Forward Rates
Bootstrapping
Term Structure Theories
  • Spot rate (z): yield on zero-coupon bond for given maturity
  • Par rate: coupon rate that prices bond at par given spot curve
  • Forward rate: interest rate for a future period, implied by spot rates
  • Yield curve: plot of YTM vs maturity for same-quality bonds
  • Normal: upward sloping; Inverted: downward; Flat; Humped
(1+z_2)^2 = (1+z_1)(1+f_{1,1})
  • Bootstrapping: extract spot rates from par bond prices sequentially
  • Start with short-term T-bill (already zero coupon)
  • Use 2-yr par bond: strip coupon cash flows, solve for 2-yr spot
  • Repeat for each maturity using previously derived spots
  • Produces spot curve consistent with observable bond prices
Par = C/(1+z_1) + (C+100)/(1+z_2)^2 → solve z_2
  • Pure expectations: forward rates = expected future spot rates
  • Liquidity preference: forward rates include liquidity premium (upward bias)
  • Preferred habitat: investors prefer certain maturities; supply/demand drives shape
  • Segmented market: each maturity segment independent
  • Most empirical support: liquidity preference + preferred habitat
Reading 58 · Fixed Income
Interest Rate Risk and Return
Sources of Return
Coupon Reinvestment vs Price Risk
Investment Horizon & Duration
  • Coupon interest: periodic payments received
  • Reinvestment income: interest earned on reinvested coupons
  • Capital gain/loss: difference between purchase price and sale/maturity price
  • Total return depends on all three sources
  • YTM realized only if coupons reinvested at YTM and held to maturity
  • Price risk: rate rises → price falls (hurts if selling early)
  • Reinvestment risk: rate falls → reinvestment income lower (hurts long-term holders)
  • These two risks offset each other at Macaulay duration horizon
  • Short horizon: price risk dominates
  • Long horizon: reinvestment risk dominates
  • Macaulay duration = investment horizon where price and reinvestment risks exactly offset
  • If horizon = Macaulay duration → immunized against parallel rate shifts
  • Coupon bond: Macaulay duration < maturity
  • Zero-coupon: Macaulay duration = maturity (no reinvestment risk)
  • Higher YTM → lower Macaulay duration
Reading 59 · Fixed Income
Yield-Based Bond Duration
Macaulay Duration
Modified Duration
Duration Factors
  • Macaulay duration: weighted average time to receive cash flows (in years)
  • Weights = PV(CF_t) / Bond Price
  • Measures time dimension of bond
  • Zero-coupon bond: MacDur = maturity
  • Coupon bond: MacDur < maturity
MacDur = ∑[t × PV(CF_t)] / Price
  • Modified duration (ModDur): % price change per 1% yield change
  • More practical than Macaulay for price sensitivity
  • Approximation — exact only for small yield changes
  • Portfolio duration = weighted average of individual durations
ModDur = MacDur / (1 + YTM/m)
%ΔP ≈ −ModDur × Δy
  • Duration increases with: longer maturity, lower coupon, lower YTM
  • Duration decreases with: shorter maturity, higher coupon, higher YTM, more frequent coupons
  • Callable bonds: duration decreases when rates fall (call becomes likely)
  • Floating-rate bonds: low duration (reset to par at each reset)
Reading 60 · Fixed Income
Yield-Based Bond Convexity
Convexity Concept
Convexity Adjustment
Negative Convexity
  • Convexity: curvature of price-yield relationship
  • Duration is linear approximation; convexity corrects for curvature
  • Positive convexity: price rises more than it falls for equal yield changes
  • Higher convexity = more desirable (better price appreciation)
  • Longer maturity and lower coupon → higher convexity
  • Full price change includes duration + convexity adjustment
  • Convexity adjustment is always positive for option-free bonds
  • Important for larger yield changes
%ΔP ≈ −ModDur × Δy + ½ × Convexity × (Δy)²
Convexity = ∑[t(t+1) PV(CF_t)] / [P(1+y)²]
  • Negative convexity: price rises less than it falls — seen in callable bonds at low yields
  • When rates fall: call option likely exercised → price capped
  • When rates rise: callable behaves like option-free (negative convexity less relevant)
  • MBS/prepayable bonds: also exhibit negative convexity
  • Investors demand higher yield for negative convexity

Curve-Based and Empirical Risk Measures
Key Rate Duration
PV01 / DV01
Empirical Duration
  • Key rate duration (KRD): sensitivity to change in yield at specific maturity point
  • Sum of KRDs = effective duration
  • Captures non-parallel yield curve shifts
  • Different bonds have different KRD profiles (e.g., bullet vs barbell)
  • Essential for portfolio immunization against curve twists
  • PV01 (Price Value of a Basis Point): dollar change in price for 1 bp yield change
  • Also called DV01 (Dollar Value of 01)
  • Useful for absolute dollar risk management
  • Basis for hedge ratio calculation
PV01 = ModDur × Price × 0.0001
Hedge ratio = PV01(bond) / PV01(futures)
  • Empirical duration: estimated from historical price-yield data (regression)
  • More accurate for bonds with embedded options (callable, MBS)
  • Analytical duration may overstate sensitivity for callables
  • Empirical duration better reflects actual market behavior
  • Limitation: relies on historical data, may not be forward-looking

Credit Risk
Expected Loss Framework
Credit Spread
Credit Migration Risk
  • Default risk: probability issuer fails to pay interest or principal
  • PD: probability of default
  • LGD: loss given default = 1 − recovery rate
  • Expected loss (EL) = PD × LGD
  • Recovery rate varies by seniority (secured > senior unsecured > sub)
EL = PD × LGD = PD × (1 − Recovery Rate)
  • Credit spread: yield premium over risk-free rate
  • Compensates for default risk, liquidity risk, and tax treatment
  • Spread duration: price sensitivity to spread changes (≈ modified duration for fixed-rate bonds)
  • Higher spread → lower bond price → higher yield
  • Spread widening = credit deterioration signal
Yield = Risk-free rate + Credit spread
  • Credit migration risk: risk of rating downgrade/upgrade
  • Downgrade → spread widens → price falls
  • Rating transitions tracked in migration matrices
  • Event risk: sudden credit deterioration (LBO, fraud, natural disaster)
  • Crossover credits: near IG/HY boundary — higher migration risk

Fixed-Income Securitization
Securitization Process
SPE / SPV Structure
Tranching & Waterfall
  • Securitization: pooling illiquid assets → issuing tradable securities
  • Originates from loans (mortgages, auto, credit card)
  • Converts illiquid loans to liquid ABS/MBS
  • Allows originators to free up capital (originate-to-distribute)
  • Benefits: lower funding costs, risk transfer, broader investor base
  • SPE/SPV (special purpose entity/vehicle): bankruptcy-remote entity holds assets
  • Isolates assets from originator's credit risk
  • True sale: assets legally transferred to SPE
  • Servicer: collects payments, manages delinquencies (often originator)
  • Trustee: protects investors' interests
  • Tranching: divides pool into classes with different risk/return
  • Senior tranche: paid first, lowest risk/yield
  • Subordinated tranches: absorb losses first, higher yield
  • Equity/residual tranche: last paid, highest risk
  • Waterfall: payment priority structure among tranches

ABS Instrument and Market Features
Types of ABS
Prepayment Risk
Credit Enhancement
  • Auto loan ABS: backed by auto loans; relatively short duration
  • Credit card ABS: revolving credit; lockout period (no principal pay-down early)
  • Student loan ABS: backed by student loans; long duration; may have gov guarantee
  • Equipment ABS: machinery, aircraft, etc.
  • CLO: collateralized loan obligation — leveraged loans
  • Prepayment risk: borrowers repay principal early
  • Contraction risk: rates fall → prepayments accelerate → shorter duration than expected
  • Extension risk: rates rise → prepayments slow → longer duration than expected
  • Contraction hurts when rates fall (reinvest at lower rates)
  • Extension hurts when rates rise (locked in at lower rates)
  • Internal credit enhancement: subordination (senior/sub structure), over-collateralization, excess spread
  • External credit enhancement: bond insurance, bank letter of credit, third-party guarantee
  • Excess spread: yield on assets minus costs — first loss buffer
  • Reserve fund: cash held to cover losses
  • More enhancement → higher rating on senior tranche

MBS Instrument and Market Features
Mortgage Pools & Pass-Throughs
PSA Prepayment Model
CMO Tranches & IO/PO Strips
  • Mortgage pass-through: pool of mortgages; investors receive pro-rata share of all cash flows
  • Agency MBS: issued/guaranteed by Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, Ginnie Mae
  • Non-agency MBS: private label — no government guarantee
  • WAC (weighted average coupon) and WAM (weighted average maturity)
  • Prepayments passed through to investors immediately
  • PSA model: industry benchmark for prepayment speeds
  • 100% PSA: CPR rises linearly from 0% to 6% over 30 months, then stays at 6%
  • 200% PSA = twice as fast prepayment; 50% PSA = half speed
  • CPR (conditional prepayment rate): annualized prepayment rate
  • SMM (single monthly mortality): monthly prepayment rate
SMM = 1 − (1 − CPR)^(1/12)
  • CMO (collateralized mortgage obligation): restructures MBS cash flows into tranches
  • Sequential pay: tranches paid off in order (A first, then B, etc.)
  • PAC tranche: planned amortization class — stable cash flows over prepayment range
  • Support/companion tranche: absorbs excess prepayments to protect PAC
  • IO strip: receives only interest — value rises when prepayments slow
  • PO strip: receives only principal — value rises when prepayments accelerate

Derivative Instrument Features
Derivative Definitions
Exchange-Traded vs OTC
Underlying Assets
  • Derivative: instrument whose value depends on underlying asset
  • Forward: obligation to buy/sell at set price on future date

  • Currencies: FX forwards, cross-currency swaps
  • Commodities: oil, gold, agricultural
  • Credit: credit default swaps (CDS)
  • Volatility: VIX futures, variance swaps

Forward Commitment and Contingent Claim Features
Forward Commitments
Contingent Claims (Options)
Moneyness & Exercise Styles
  • Forward commitment: both parties obligated to transact (forwards, futures, swaps)
  • Long forward: obligated to buy — profits when price rises
  • Short forward: obligated to sell — profits when price falls
  • Swaps: series of forward contracts; counterparties exchange cash flows periodically
  • Zero-sum: gains to one party = losses to other
  • Contingent claim: payoff contingent on future event (options)
  • Call option: right to buy at strike price X
  • Put option: right to sell at strike price X
  • Option buyer pays premium; seller receives premium
  • Buyer's max loss = premium paid; seller's max loss = potentially unlimited (call)
  • In-the-money (ITM): exercise has positive intrinsic value
  • At-the-money (ATM): S = X
  • Out-of-the-money (OTM): exercise has negative intrinsic value
  • European option: exercisable only at expiration
  • American option: exercisable any time before expiration
  • Bermudan option: exercisable on specific dates

Reading 71 · Derivatives
Arbitrage, Replication, and Cost of Carry
No-Arbitrage Principle
Replication
Cost of Carry Model
  • Law of one price: identical cash flows must have same price
  • If mispriced: arbitrageurs buy cheap, sell expensive → prices converge
  • Arbitrage profits should be riskless and require zero net investment
  • No-arbitrage condition establishes forward/futures fair value
  • Markets generally efficient — arbitrage opportunities short-lived
  • Replication: creating same payoff using different instruments
  • Forward = Long spot + Borrowing (carry position to delivery)
  • Replication used to price derivatives based on no-arbitrage
  • Dynamic replication: continuously adjust hedge to replicate option payoff
  • Risk-neutral pricing: price = PV(expected payoffs) under risk-neutral measure
  • Cost of carry: net cost of holding underlying to delivery date
  • Includes: financing cost, storage, insurance
  • Less: convenience yield (benefit of holding physical), dividends
  • Continuous: F = S × e^((r−q)T)
  • Discrete: F = S × (1+r−q)^T
F = S × e^((r−q)T) [continuous]
F = S × (1+r−q)^T [discrete]
Reading 72 · Derivatives
Pricing Forward Contracts
Forward Price Formula
Value of Forward Contract
Types of Forwards
  • Forward price (F): price agreed today for delivery at T
  • Set so contract has zero value at inception
  • For stock paying dividends: adjust spot for PV of dividends
  • For currency: use interest rate parity
F_0 = S_0 × (1+r)^T − FV(Dividends)
F_0(FX) = S_0 × (1+r_d)^T / (1+r_f)^T
  • At inception: V = 0 (fair priced forward)
  • During life: V = PV of (F_t − F_0) for long position
  • At expiration: V = S_T − F_0 (long) or F_0 − S_T (short)
  • Long profits if S_T > F_0; short profits if S_T < F_0
V_t(long) = S_t − F_0/(1+r)^(T−t)
  • Equity forward: on stock or index; adjust for dividends
  • FX forward: exchange currencies at future date; use interest rate parity
  • Fixed income forward: on bond; adjust for coupon cash flows
  • FRA (forward rate agreement): on interest rate; settled in cash
  • All types follow same no-arbitrage logic
Reading 73 · Derivatives
Pricing Futures Contracts
Futures vs Forward Pricing
Marking to Market & Basis
Convergence
  • Futures and forwards have same price if interest rates are non-stochastic
  • If rates are positively correlated with asset: futures price > forward price
  • If rates are negatively correlated: futures price < forward price
  • In practice, difference is small — often assumed equal
  • Futures require margin; forwards do not
  • Marking to market: daily settlement of gains/losses
  • Eliminates accumulated credit risk (unlike forwards)
  • Initial margin: deposit required to open position
  • Maintenance margin: minimum balance; margin call if below
  • Basis = Spot price − Futures price (can also be reversed)
  • Basis risk: spot and futures may not move perfectly together
Basis = S − F
  • Convergence: futures price converges to spot price at expiration
  • If F > S at expiry: arbitrage by selling futures, buying spot
  • If F < S at expiry: arbitrage by buying futures, selling spot
  • Calendar spread: difference between two futures contracts of same commodity, different expiry
  • Cost of carry drives shape of futures term structure
Reading 74 · Derivatives
Pricing Interest Rate and Other Swaps
Plain Vanilla IRS Structure
Swap as Bond Portfolio
Swap Rate Calculation
  • Plain vanilla IRS: pay fixed, receive floating (or vice versa)
  • Notional principal is not exchanged
  • Net payment made each period
  • Fixed rate payer: benefits when floating rates rise
  • Floating rate payer: benefits when floating rates fall
  • Swap tenor, reset frequency, notional specified in agreement
  • Pay-fixed IRS = Long floating-rate bond + Short fixed-rate bond
  • Receive-fixed IRS = Long fixed-rate bond + Short floating-rate bond
  • Swap value = difference in value of the two bond positions
  • At inception: swap value = 0 (both legs equal in PV)
  • During life: value changes as rates change
V_swap(pay fixed) = V_float − V_fixed
  • Swap rate: fixed rate making swap value = 0 at inception
  • Solve for fixed rate (C) such that PV(fixed) = PV(floating)
  • PV(floating) = 1 (par, since FRN = par on reset)
  • C = (1 − Z_N) / ∑Z_t where Z_t are discount factors

Option Replication Using Put-Call Parity
Put-Call Parity
Protective Put & Fiduciary Call
Synthetic Positions
  • Fundamental relationship between European put, call, stock, and bond
  • Violation creates arbitrage opportunity
  • Applies to European options only (strictly)
  • If LHS > RHS: sell protected put, buy fiduciary call
  • If LHS < RHS: buy protective put, sell fiduciary call
P + S = C + PV(X)
C − P = S − PV(X)
  • Protective put: S + P — stock + put = portfolio insurance
  • Limits downside while preserving upside
  • Fiduciary call: C + PV(X) — call + risk-free bond
  • Same payoff as protective put at all stock prices
  • Put-call parity: protective put = fiduciary call
  • Synthetic call: C = P + S − PV(X)
  • Synthetic put: P = C − S + PV(X)
  • Synthetic stock: S = C − P + PV(X)
  • Synthetic bond: PV(X) = P + S − C
  • Allows replication using other instruments
  • Used for arbitrage, hedging, and synthetic position creation

Valuing Derivatives Using One-Period Binomial Model
Up/Down Factors
Risk-Neutral Probabilities
Call & Put Valuation
  • Stock moves to one of two values in next period
  • u (up factor): S_u = u × S_0 where u > 1
  • d (down factor): S_d = d × S_0 where d < 1
  • No-arbitrage requires: d < (1+r) < u
  • Typical: u = e^(σ√T), d = e^(−σ√T) = 1/u (CRR model)
S_u = u × S_0 ; S_d = d × S_0
  • Risk-neutral probability (π): not the real probability — used for pricing
  • Probability such that expected return = risk-free rate
  • πu = (1+r − d) / (u − d)
  • πd = 1 − πu
  • Option value = PV of risk-neutral expected payoff
πu = (1+r − d) / (u − d)
  • Call value: C_0 = [π_u × C_u + π_d × C_d] / (1+r)
  • Put value: P_0 = [π_u × P_u + π_d × P_d] / (1+r)
  • C_u = max(S_u − X, 0); C_d = max(S_d − X, 0)
  • P_u = max(X − S_u, 0); P_d = max(X − S_d, 0)
  • Extend to multi-period by backward induction
C_0 = [π_u C_u + π_d C_d] / (1+r)
