Portfolio Management · Reading 20
20. Portfolio Risk and Return: Part I
Return Measures
Risk Measures
Portfolio Diversification
  • HPR: total return including income and price change
  • HPR = (P₁ − P₀ + D) / P₀
  • Arithmetic mean: simple average; overstates compound growth
  • Geometric mean (CAGR): correct measure of wealth growth over multiple periods
  • G = [(1+R₁)(1+R₂)…(1+Rₙ)]^(1/n) − 1
  • Money-weighted return: IRR method; accounts for cash flow timing; investor-specific
  • Time-weighted return: eliminates external cash flow impact; preferred for manager evaluation
  • Variance: average squared deviation from mean
  • σ² = Σ[Pᵢ(Rᵢ − E[R])²]
  • Standard deviation: √variance; same units as return
  • Covariance: how two assets move together; positive or negative
  • Cov(A,B) = ρ(A,B) × σ_A × σ_B
  • Correlation (ρ): standardized covariance; ranges −1 to +1
  • ρ = −1: perfect negative (max diversification); ρ = +1: no diversification benefit
  • Portfolio variance (2 assets):
  • σ²_p = w²_Aσ²_A + w²_Bσ²_B + 2w_Aw_BCov(A,B)
  • Low correlation → portfolio risk below weighted-average risk
  • Efficient frontier: highest return for given risk; optimal risky portfolios
  • MVP: minimum variance portfolio; leftmost point on frontier
  • Diversifiable (unsystematic) risk: firm-specific; eliminated by diversification
  • Systematic risk: market-wide; cannot be diversified; compensated by market premium
Portfolio Management · Reading 21
21. Portfolio Risk and Return: Part II
Capital Market Line (CML)
CAPM & SML
Beta & Performance Measures
  • CML: adds risk-free asset to efficient frontier; all investors hold market portfolio
  • E(Rp) = Rf + [(E(Rm)−Rf)/σm] × σp
  • Separation theorem: optimal risky portfolio (market) is same for all; only Rf vs. market allocation differs by risk aversion
  • CML uses total risk (σ); only efficient portfolios lie on CML
  • Borrowing (levered) portfolios extend beyond market portfolio on CML
  • CAPM: required return based on systematic risk only
  • E(Ri) = Rf + βi × [E(Rm) − Rf]
  • SML: plots expected return vs. beta; all fairly priced assets on SML
  • Above SML → underpriced (positive alpha → buy)
  • Below SML → overpriced (negative alpha → sell)
  • Market risk premium = E(Rm) − Rf
  • SML applies to any asset; CML only to efficient portfolios
  • Beta: systematic risk sensitivity
  • β = Cov(Ri,Rm) / σ²m = ρ(i,m) × σi/σm
  • Jensen's alpha: actual − CAPM required; α > 0 = outperformance
  • Sharpe ratio: excess return per unit total risk; use for entire portfolio
  • Sharpe = (Rp − Rf) / σp
  • Treynor ratio: excess return per unit beta; use for diversified portfolio
  • Treynor = (Rp − Rf) / βp
  • M² measure: risk-adjusts portfolio to market's total risk for direct comparison
Corporate Issuers · Reading 22
22. Organizational Forms, Corporate Issuer Features & Ownership
Business Structures
Corporate Features
Ownership Types
  • Sole proprietorship: unlimited personal liability; owner = manager; no separate taxation
  • General partnership: all partners share unlimited liability; pass-through taxation
  • Limited partnership (LP): GP has unlimited liability; LPs capped at investment; common in PE/hedge funds
  • Corporation: separate legal entity; limited liability; double taxation (corporate + dividend); perpetual existence
  • LLC/LLP: limited liability + pass-through taxation hybrid
  • Common equity: residual claim; voting rights; highest risk/return
  • Preferred shares: fixed dividend; priority over common in liquidation; typically no voting
  • Debt: contractual obligation; interest tax-deductible; priority in bankruptcy
  • Convertible bonds: can convert to equity; carry lower coupon than straight debt
  • Financial leverage: debt amplifies equity returns — increases both upside and downside
  • Public companies: exchange-listed; regulatory disclosure; more liquid but costly
  • Private companies: no public trading; financed by PE/VC/retained earnings; less disclosure
  • Controlling vs. minority shareholders: controlling can influence strategy; minority face expropriation risk
  • Shareholder vs. creditor conflict: shareholders prefer higher risk; creditors prefer stability
  • Dual-class shares: founders retain voting control despite minority economic ownership

23. Investors and Other Stakeholders
Principal-Agent Problem
Stakeholder Groups
ESG Considerations
  • Principal-agent problem: managers (agents) may not act in owners' (principals') interests
  • Manager-owner conflict: empire-building, excessive perks, risk-avoidance vs. value maximization
  • Shareholder-creditor conflict: shareholders benefit from risk-taking; creditors bear downside (asset substitution)
  • Agency costs: monitoring + bonding + residual loss
  • Board of directors: primary mechanism aligning manager and shareholder interests
  • Shareholders: residual claimants; seek long-term value; vote on major decisions
  • Creditors/bondholders: contractual rights; protected by covenants
  • Employees: human capital at risk; interested in job security and wages
  • Customers: quality, fair pricing; long-term relationship value
  • Suppliers: trade credit exposure; interested in payment reliability
  • Government/regulators: tax revenue, compliance, employment
  • ESG: framework for non-financial risk and opportunity evaluation
  • Environmental: carbon emissions, climate risk, resource use — affects costs and regulatory exposure
  • Social: labor practices, supply chain ethics, diversity and inclusion
  • Governance: board independence, executive pay, transparency, anti-corruption
  • ESG integration: negative screening, positive tilts, engagement, impact investing
  • Poor ESG → reputational risk, fines, stranded assets, higher cost of capital

24. Corporate Governance: Conflicts, Mechanisms, Risks & Benefits
Board Structure
Governance Mechanisms
Risks & Benefits
  • Independent directors: no material ties to company; majority should be independent
  • One-tier board: executive + non-executive directors; common in US/UK
  • Two-tier board: supervisory board oversees management board; common in Germany
  • Board committees: audit, compensation, nominations — all should be independent
  • Staggered boards: directors elected in tranches; makes hostile takeovers harder
  • Internal mechanisms: board oversight, performance-linked pay, internal audit, shareholder voting
  • External mechanisms: market for corporate control (takeover threat), auditors, regulators, proxy advisors
  • Proxy voting: vote without attending; institutional investors increasingly active
  • Shareholder activism: push for strategic/governance changes
  • Takeover defenses: poison pills, supermajority requirements — can entrench management
  • Say-on-pay: non-binding shareholder vote on executive compensation
  • Governance risks: fraud, value destruction, excessive risk-taking, tunneling (related-party abuse)
  • Concentration risk: controlling shareholder may expropriate minority
  • Benefits of good governance: lower cost of capital, better financing access, reduced agency costs
  • Long-termism vs. short-termism: quarterly pressure vs. sustainable value creation
  • Key frameworks: Sarbanes-Oxley (US), UK Corporate Governance Code, OECD Principles

25. Working Capital and Liquidity
Liquidity Ratios
Cash Conversion Cycle
Liquidity Management
  • Current ratio: ability to cover short-term obligations
  • Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities
  • Quick ratio: excludes inventory (least liquid)
  • Quick = (Cash + Securities + Receivables) / CL
  • Cash ratio: most conservative; only cash and equivalents
  • Cash Ratio = (Cash + Mkt Securities) / CL
  • Interpret vs. industry norms and historical trends
  • CCC: net days cash tied up in operations; shorter = more efficient
  • CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO
  • DIO: avg days to sell inventory
  • DIO = (Avg Inventory / COGS) × 365
  • DSO: avg days to collect receivables
  • DSO = (Avg Receivables / Revenue) × 365
  • DPO: avg days to pay suppliers; longer = uses supplier financing
  • Negative CCC: collect cash before paying (e.g., Amazon)
  • Primary liquidity sources: cash balances, revolving credit, trade credit — readily available
  • Secondary sources: asset liquidation, debt restructuring, equity issuance — costly and slow
  • Drags on liquidity: slow receivables, obsolete inventory — delay cash inflows
  • Pulls on liquidity: paying early, reduced credit lines — accelerate outflows
  • Working capital = CA − CL; positive = short-term buffer
  • Excess WC is costly; insufficient WC is risky — balance is key

26. Capital Investments and Capital Allocation
NPV & IRR
Capital Budgeting Process
Real Options
  • NPV rule: invest if NPV > 0; maximizes shareholder wealth; theoretically superior
  • NPV = Σ[CFₜ/(1+r)ᵗ] − Initial Investment
  • IRR: rate where NPV = 0; accept if IRR > hurdle rate
  • NPV vs IRR conflicts: choose NPV for mutually exclusive projects (scale/timing differences)
  • MIRR: reinvestment at WACC; resolves reinvestment rate problem
  • Payback period: years to recover investment; ignores TVM and post-payback flows
  • Profitability Index (PI) = NPV / Investment; useful for capital rationing
  • Expansion projects: new ventures; use incremental after-tax operating cash flows
  • Replacement projects: use differential cash flows (new minus old)
  • Regulatory/safety projects: mandated; evaluate on cost minimization
  • Sunk costs: already incurred; irrelevant to decisions
  • Opportunity costs: foregone value of best alternative; must be included
  • Cannibalization: negative externality — reduce sales of existing products; must be captured
  • Use incremental after-tax cash flows, not accounting income
  • Real options: right to take future action; adds value beyond static NPV
  • Abandonment option: exit project; limits downside (like a put option)
  • Expansion option: scale up if successful (like a call option)
  • Flexibility option: change inputs/outputs; reduces cost risk
  • Timing option: delay until more info available; valuable in uncertainty
  • Fundamental option: embedded in project nature (e.g., only drill if oil price high)
  • NPV with real options = static NPV + option value

27. Capital Structure
MM Theorems
WACC & Optimal Structure
Leverage Effects
  • MM I (no taxes): capital structure irrelevant; firm value independent of financing mix
  • MM II (no taxes): cost of equity rises with leverage; WACC stays constant
  • re = r₀ + (r₀ − rd) × (D/E)
  • MM with taxes: interest tax-deductible → tax shield adds value
  • V_L = V_U + (T × D)
  • Financial distress costs: offset tax shield at high leverage — direct (legal) + indirect (lost customers)
  • Trade-off theory: optimal structure balances tax shield vs. distress costs
  • WACC: minimum required return on assets; discount rate for projects
  • WACC = (E/V)×re + (D/V)×rd×(1−T)
  • Optimal structure: minimizes WACC and maximizes firm value
  • Pecking order theory: prefer retained earnings → debt → equity; new equity signals overvaluation
  • Static trade-off theory: target leverage based on tax/distress tradeoff
  • As leverage rises: tax shield captured, but distress probability increases
  • Business risk: variability of EBIT; driven by demand, operating leverage, competition
  • Financial risk: additional equity risk from debt use
  • DOL: % change in EBIT per 1% change in sales
  • DOL = (Sales − VC) / EBIT
  • DFL: % change in EPS per 1% change in EBIT
  • DFL = EBIT / (EBIT − Interest)
  • DTL = DOL × DFL; combined earnings volatility
  • High DOL + High DFL = highly volatile EPS — risky for equity investors

28. Business Models
Value Creation
Revenue Models
Business Model Types
  • Value proposition: what customers find valuable — quality, price, convenience, innovation
  • Value chain: sequence from inputs to delivery; each step must add more value than it costs
  • Competitive advantage: cost leadership, differentiation, or niche focus
  • Network effects: value grows with more users; creates strong moat (social media, exchanges)
  • Switching costs: costs to change providers; increases retention and pricing power
  • Economies of scale: unit costs fall as volume increases
  • Subscription: recurring payments; predictable revenue; high LTV (Netflix, SaaS)
  • Transaction/volume-based: revenue per unit; variable and activity-driven
  • Fee-for-service: payment for specific services; consulting, healthcare, legal
  • Licensing: charge for IP use (patents, software, brand); high margin, scalable
  • Advertising: free to users; monetize via advertisers (Google, Meta)
  • Freemium: basic free, premium paid; low user acquisition cost
  • B2B: sell to companies; longer sales cycles, larger contracts, relationship-driven
  • B2C: sell to end users; volume-based, brand important
  • Platform/marketplace: connects buyers and sellers; earns fees; benefits from network effects (Airbnb, Uber)
  • Franchise: franchisor provides brand/systems; franchisee pays royalties; capital-light for franchisor
  • Vertically integrated: controls supply chain; reduces dependency but increases capex
  • Asset-light: outsources capital-intensive functions; higher ROE but less control
