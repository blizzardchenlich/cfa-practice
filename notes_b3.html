Expense Recognition & Non-Recurring Items
EPS & Common-Size IS
  • Step 1: Identify the contract with customer
  • Step 2: Identify performance obligations
  • Step 3: Determine transaction price

R31 — Analyzing Balance Sheets
Asset Classification
Liabilities & Equity
Liquidity Ratios & B/S Analysis
  • Current assets: converted to cash within 1 year (cash, AR, inventory, prepaid)
  • Non-current assets: PP&E, intangibles, goodwill, long-term investments

  • Non-current liabilities: long-term debt, deferred tax, lease obligations
  • Shareholders' equity: contributed capital + retained earnings + AOCI + treasury stock (contra)
  • AOCI: unrealized G/L on AFS securities, FX translation, pension adjustments
Retained Earnings = Prior RE + NI − Dividends

R32 — Analyzing Statements of Cash Flows I
Direct vs Indirect Method
Operating Cash Flow
CFO Adjustments from Net Income
  • Direct method: lists actual cash receipts and payments from operations; preferred by standard-setters
  • Indirect method: starts with net income, adjusts for non-cash items and working capital changes; more common in practice
  • Both methods produce the same CFO total
  • Investing and financing sections are identical under both methods
  • IFRS: interest paid can be CFO or CFF; dividends received can be CFO or CFI
  • US GAAP: interest paid = CFO; dividends received = CFO
  • CFO (Indirect): NI + non-cash charges − non-cash gains ± working capital changes
  • Non-cash add-backs: depreciation, amortization, impairment losses
  • Working capital increases in assets = cash outflow (subtract)
  • Working capital increases in liabilities = cash inflow (add)
CFO = NI + D&A ± WC Changes ± Other
AdjustmentEffect on CFO

Inventory increases− (cash paid for stock)
AP increases+ (cash not yet paid)
Depreciation+ (non-cash expense)
Gain on sale asset− (goes to CFI)
  • Gains on asset sales are removed from CFO and shown in CFI

R33 — Analyzing Statements of Cash Flows II
Investing & Financing Cash Flows
Free Cash Flow (FCFF & FCFE)
Cash Flow Analysis Ratios
  • CFI: purchases/sales of PP&E, investments, acquisitions
  • Capex: major use of cash in CFI; purchase of PP&E is outflow
  • CFF: debt issuance/repayment, equity issuance/buybacks, dividends paid
  • IFRS: dividends paid can be CFO or CFF (US GAAP: always CFF)
  • Net increase/decrease in cash = CFO + CFI + CFF
Ending Cash = Beg Cash + CFO + CFI + CFF
  • FCFF (Free CF to Firm): CF available to all capital providers
  • FCFE (Free CF to Equity): CF available to equity holders after debt obligations
FCFF = CFO + Int(1−t) − Capex FCFE = CFO − Capex + Net Borrowing
  • Positive FCF indicates company can fund growth internally
  • Capex = ending PP&E − beginning PP&E + depreciation
CFO-to-Revenue = CFO / Revenue CFO-to-Net Income = CFO / NI Debt Coverage = CFO / Total Debt Capex Coverage = CFO / Capex
  • CFO > Net Income consistently = higher earnings quality
  • Large gap between NI and CFO may signal aggressive accruals
  • Dividend coverage: CFO / dividends paid

R35 — Analysis of Long-Term Assets
Capitalization vs Expensing
Depreciation Methods
Impairment, Revaluation & Intangibles
  • Capitalize: costs added to asset on BS; expensed over time via depreciation
  • Expense: costs charged immediately to IS; reduces current period income
  • Capitalizing increases: assets, equity, NI (short-term); lowers future NI via depreciation
  • Research costs: always expensed (IFRS & GAAP)
  • Development costs: IFRS = capitalize if 6 criteria met; GAAP = expense (except internal-use software)
Asset Turnover = Revenue / Avg Total Assets
  • Straight-line (SL): equal annual depreciation
  • Double-declining balance (DDB): accelerated; higher early depreciation
  • Units of production: based on actual usage/output
SL Dep = (Cost − Salvage) / Useful Life DDB Dep = (2 / Life) × Book Value UoP Dep = (Cost − Salvage) / Total Units × Units Used
  • DDB switches to SL when SL gives higher charge
  • Accelerated depreciation: lower early NI, higher CFO (same cash flow effect)
  • Impairment: carrying value exceeds recoverable amount; write down to recoverable amount
  • IFRS: recoverable = higher of fair value less costs to sell OR value in use
  • GAAP: impairment if carrying value > undiscounted future CF; write down to fair value
  • Revaluation (IFRS only): can write PP&E up to fair value; increase goes to revaluation surplus (OCI)
  • Goodwill: not amortized; tested annually for impairment
  • Intangibles acquired separately: recognized at cost; internally generated: generally expensed

R36 — Topics in Long-Term Liabilities and Equity
Bonds Payable & Amortization
Leases: Finance vs Operating
Shareholders' Equity Components
  • Issued at par: coupon rate = market rate; no premium/discount
  • Premium bond: coupon > market rate; amortize premium over life
  • Discount bond: coupon < market rate; amortize discount over life
  • Effective interest method: interest expense = carrying value × market rate
Int Expense = Carrying Value × Market Rate Amortization = Int Expense − Coupon Paid New CV = Old CV + Discount Amort (or − Premium Amort)
  • Finance lease (lessee): asset & liability on BS; front-loaded total expense (int + dep > rent in early years)
  • Operating lease (lessee, old GAAP): rent expense; off-balance sheet
  • Under IFRS 16 / ASC 842: all leases > 12 months on BS as ROU asset + lease liability
  • Finance lease: higher early expense, lower later; operating lease: straight-line
  • Finance lease: higher D/E ratio, lower asset turnover initially
Lease Liability = PV of future lease payments
  • Contributed capital: par value + additional paid-in capital (APIC)
  • Retained earnings: cumulative undistributed net income
  • Treasury stock: shares repurchased; reduces equity (contra-equity)
  • AOCI: unrealized gains/losses bypass IS; recorded in OCI
  • Book value per share: total equity / shares outstanding
  • Share repurchases: reduce shares outstanding, increase EPS (if NI unchanged)
BV/Share = Total Equity / Shares Outstanding

R37 — Analysis of Income Taxes
Current vs Deferred Taxes
DTL / DTA Creation
Tax Rate Reconciliation & Valuation Allowance
  • Tax expense (IS): = current tax + change in deferred tax
  • Current tax: taxes actually payable to government for the period
  • Deferred tax: arises from temporary differences between accounting income and taxable income
  • Temporary differences: timing; will reverse in future periods
  • Permanent differences: do not create deferred taxes (e.g., tax-exempt income)
Tax Expense = Current Tax + ΔDeferred Tax Effective Tax Rate = Tax Expense / Pre-tax Income
  • DTL (Deferred Tax Liability): taxable income < accounting income now; will pay more tax later (e.g., accelerated depreciation for tax)
  • DTA (Deferred Tax Asset): taxable income > accounting income now; will save taxes later (e.g., warranty expense recognized early on IS)
  • DTL: recognize always; DTA: recognize if probable to utilize
DTL = Temp Diff × Tax Rate (tax base < carrying value) DTA = Temp Diff × Tax Rate (tax base > carrying value)
  • Statutory rate: legal tax rate in jurisdiction
  • Effective rate: actual tax paid / pre-tax income; may differ due to permanent diffs
  • Tax rate reconciliation: explains difference between statutory and effective rate
  • Valuation allowance (GAAP): reduces DTA if not likely to be realized; increase in allowance = higher tax expense
  • IFRS uses "probable" threshold; no separate valuation allowance account

R38 — Financial Reporting Quality
Quality Spectrum
Earnings Quality
Red Flags & Aggressive Accounting
  • High quality reporting: compliant with GAAP/IFRS, decision-useful, sustainable earnings
  • Biased accounting: compliant but uses choices to manage earnings
  • Non-compliant: fraudulent reporting, fictitious transactions
  • Quality spectrum: GAAP-compliant & decision-useful → GAAP-compliant but biased → non-compliant
  • Reporting quality (compliance) distinct from earnings quality (sustainability)
  • High earnings quality: recurring, sustainable, cash-backed income
  • Accruals-based earnings are less persistent than cash-based earnings
  • Accruals ratio: measures accrual component; lower = higher quality
Accruals Ratio (BS) = (NI − CFO − CFI) / Avg NOA NOA = Operating Assets − Operating Liabilities
  • Large positive accruals ratio: potential earnings management concern
  • Channel stuffing: shipping excess goods to distributors to inflate revenue
  • Bill-and-hold: recording revenue before delivery
  • Capitalizing operating expenses: inflates NI and assets
  • Cookie jar reserves: over-provisioning in good years, releasing in bad years
  • Red flags: AR growing faster than revenue, big bath charges, frequent accounting changes, CFO << NI
  • Beneish M-score: quantitative fraud detection model

R40 — Introduction to Financial Statement Modeling
Building Pro Forma Statements
Forecasting Revenue & Costs
Sensitivity & Scenario Analysis
  • Pro forma statements: projected IS, BS, and CF based on assumptions
  • Start with revenue forecast; derive COGS and operating expenses
  • Circular references: interest expense depends on debt balance which depends on net income (solved iteratively)
  • Balance sheet must balance: plug item typically retained earnings or debt
  • Quality of model depends on quality of underlying assumptions
  • Top-down forecasting: start with macro/industry outlook, derive company revenue
  • Bottom-up forecasting: start with company-level drivers (units, pricing, capacity)
  • COGS as % of revenue: common approach for cost forecasting
  • Fixed vs variable costs: fixed costs = operating leverage
  • Gross margin trends signal pricing power and cost efficiency
Revenue = Volume × Price COGS = Revenue × COGS%
  • Sensitivity analysis: change one variable at a time; observe impact on output (e.g., NI)
  • Scenario analysis: define base, bull, and bear case; changes multiple variables simultaneously
  • Monte Carlo simulation: generates distribution of outcomes using random variable inputs
  • Analysts report range of outcomes, not just point estimates
  • Key sensitivities: revenue growth rate, gross margin, discount rate

R41 — Market Organization and Structure
Types of Markets
Order Types
Short Selling Mechanics
  • Quote-driven (dealer) market: dealers post bid/ask; investors trade against dealer inventory
  • Order-driven market: buy/sell orders matched directly; no dealer intermediary (e.g., electronic exchanges)
  • Brokered market: broker finds counterparty; common for illiquid assets
  • Primary market: new issuance of securities (IPO, secondary offerings)
  • Secondary market: trading of existing securities
  • Bid-ask spread: dealer's profit; narrower = more liquid
  • Market order: execute immediately at best available price; price uncertainty
  • Limit order: specify max buy price or min sell price; execution uncertainty
  • Stop order (stop-loss): becomes market order when price hits trigger
  • Stop-limit order: becomes limit order at trigger; may not execute
  • Day order: expires end of trading day
  • GTC (Good-till-cancelled): remains until filled or cancelled
  • Short selling: borrow shares, sell now, buy back later at lower price
  • Profit = sell price − repurchase price − borrowing costs − dividends paid
  • Margin requirement: short seller must post collateral; margin call if price rises
  • Maximum loss: theoretically unlimited (price can rise indefinitely)
  • Maximum gain: limited to initial sale price (stock can only fall to zero)
  • Uptick rule (GAAP historical): short only on uptick to prevent downward spirals
Profit = Sale Price − Repurchase Price − Costs

R42 — Security Market Indexes
Weighting Methods
Rebalancing & Reconstitution
Uses of Indexes
  • Price-weighted: average of prices; higher-priced stocks have more influence (e.g., DJIA)
  • Value-weighted (market cap): weight = market cap / total market cap (e.g., S&P 500, MSCI); float-adjusted version uses free float
  • Equal-weighted: each security has same weight; requires frequent rebalancing; small-cap bias
  • Fundamental-weighted: weight by earnings, dividends, sales, book value
PW Index = ∑Prices / Adjusted Divisor VW Weight = Mkt Cap_i / ∑Mkt Cap
  • Rebalancing: restoring weights to target; required more often for equal-weighted indexes
  • Reconstitution: adding/removing securities from the index; can cause price pressure on additions
  • Price-weighted: adjust divisor for stock splits to maintain continuity
  • Value-weighted: automatically adjusts for splits and dividends (self-adjusting)
  • Return index: includes dividends (total return); price index: price changes only
  • Gauge of market sentiment and economic conditions
  • Benchmark for active portfolio manager performance
  • Basis for passive/index funds and ETFs
  • Proxy for systematic risk in asset pricing models (CAPM beta)
  • Asset allocation tools: factor indexes (value, growth, momentum)
  • Survivorship bias: indexes that exclude failed companies overstate historical returns

EMH: Weak / Semi-Strong / Strong
Anomalies

FormInformation ReflectedImplication

  • Markets are generally semi-strong efficient in developed markets
  • Abnormal return = actual return − expected return (CAPM)
  • Calendar anomalies: January effect (small caps outperform in Jan), day-of-week effect
  • Momentum: past winners continue winning short-term
  • Value anomaly: low P/E, low P/B stocks outperform on average

  • Herding: following the crowd; can create bubbles and crashes

  • Representativeness: judging based on superficial similarities
  • Behavioral biases explain persistent anomalies that rational models cannot

R44 — Overview of Equity Securities
Common vs Preferred Shares
Private Equity
Risk/Return, Book vs Market Value, ROE
  • Common shares: voting rights, residual claim, dividends not guaranteed
  • Preferred shares: fixed dividend (like debt), priority over common in liquidation, typically no voting rights
  • Callable preferred: issuer can redeem at set price
  • Convertible preferred: can convert to common shares
  • Cumulative preferred: unpaid dividends accumulate (must be paid before common dividends)
  • Participating preferred: share in extra dividends above stated rate
  • Venture capital: early-stage companies; high risk/return
  • Leveraged buyout (LBO): acquisition using significant debt; target's CF services debt
  • Private equity: illiquid; long investment horizon; higher expected returns vs public equity
  • Exit strategies: IPO, strategic sale, secondary sale
  • PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity): private placement in already-public company
  • Private equity returns include illiquidity premium
  • Equity risk premium: excess return over risk-free rate demanded by equity investors
  • Book value: total equity / shares outstanding (accounting-based)
  • Market value: share price × shares outstanding (market-based)
  • P/B > 1: market expects returns above cost of equity
ROE = Net Income / Avg Shareholders' Equity P/B = Market Price / Book Value per Share
  • Higher ROE with same P/B = better value; ROE drives P/B in theory

R45 — Company Analysis: Past and Present
FSA for Equity Analysis
Quality of Earnings Assessment
Porter's Five Forces Applied
  • Equity analysts use FSA to assess intrinsic value and investment merit
  • Key financial metrics: revenue growth, gross margin, operating margin, FCF, ROE, ROIC
  • ROIC (Return on Invested Capital): measures how well company generates returns above cost of capital
  • Compare margins over time and against competitors
  • Analyze segment data for diversified companies
ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital NOPAT = EBIT × (1 − Tax Rate)
  • Sustainable earnings: recurring, cash-backed, not dependent on one-time items
  • Remove non-recurring items to assess underlying earnings power
  • Compare CFO to Net Income: consistent CFO > NI signals high quality
  • Watch for aggressive revenue recognition, understated reserves
  • Normalizing earnings: adjust for cyclicality, non-recurring items
  • Analyst adjustments: operating leases, pension obligations, off-BS items
  • Threat of new entrants: barriers to entry (economies of scale, brand, regulation)
  • Bargaining power of suppliers: few suppliers = high power, squeeze margins
  • Bargaining power of buyers: few buyers or price-sensitive = high power
  • Threat of substitutes: alternative products limit pricing power
  • Industry rivalry: intense competition erodes profitability
  • Strong competitive position = economic moat = sustained excess returns

R46 — Industry and Competitive Analysis
Industry Classification
Porter's Five Forces
Competitive Strategy
  • GICS (Global Industry Classification Standard): 11 sectors, 24 industry groups, 69 industries, 158 sub-industries
  • SIC (Standard Industrial Classification): older US government system
  • Commercial classifiers: MSCI, S&P (GICS); FTSE Russell (ICB)
  • Business cycle sensitivity: cyclical industries (autos, materials) vs defensive (utilities, consumer staples)
  • Industry life cycle: embryonic, growth, shakeout, mature, declining
  • Force 1 — New entrants: high barriers protect incumbents (patents, scale, regulation)
  • Force 2 — Suppliers: concentrated suppliers can demand higher prices
  • Force 3 — Buyers: powerful buyers push prices down
  • Force 4 — Substitutes: close substitutes cap pricing power
  • Force 5 — Rivalry: many competitors, slow growth, high fixed costs = intense rivalry
  • All 5 forces together determine industry profitability potential
  • Cost leadership: lowest cost producer; compete on price; high volumes; Walmart, Amazon
  • Differentiation: unique product/brand; command premium price; Apple, LVMH
  • Focus strategy: target niche market with cost or differentiation approach
  • "Stuck in the middle": no clear strategy; typically earns below-average returns
  • Competitive advantage: cost advantage or pricing power sustained over time
  • Analyst evaluates whether strategy is sustainable given industry structure

R47 — Company Analysis: Forecasting
Top-Down vs Bottom-Up
Revenue Forecasting
Earnings & Cash Flow Projections
  • Top-down: macro GDP growth → industry growth → company market share → revenue
  • Bottom-up: company unit volumes, pricing, capacity → aggregate to revenue
  • Top-down better for macro-sensitive industries; bottom-up better for company-specific analysis
  • Hybrid approach: often most robust; cross-check top-down with bottom-up
  • Consider currency effects for multinational companies
  • Key drivers: market size, market share, pricing power, product mix
  • Same-store sales: organic growth excluding new locations
  • Volume × price decomposition reveals sources of revenue change
  • Use historical growth rates as starting point; adjust for strategic changes
  • Backlogs, order books, contracts: provide revenue visibility
Revenue Growth = Volume Growth + Pricing Growth Market Share = Co. Revenue / Industry Revenue
  • Forecast gross margin first (revenue − COGS%); then operating expenses
  • Operating leverage: fixed costs amplify EPS swings relative to revenue changes
  • Estimate capex based on maintenance vs growth spending
  • Working capital changes affect FCF: growing companies consume WC
EBITDA = EBIT + D&A FCF = EBITDA − Taxes − Capex − ΔWC
  • Earnings estimates drive price targets in equity research

R48 — Equity Valuation: Concepts and Basic Tools
DDM & Gordon Growth Model
Multiplier Models (P/E, P/B, P/S, P/CF)
Asset-Based Valuation
  • DDM: value = PV of all future dividends
  • Gordon Growth Model: assumes constant dividend growth forever
  • Appropriate for: stable, dividend-paying companies with predictable growth
  • Multi-stage DDM: high growth phase then stable growth; sum PVs of each stage
  • If g ≥ r: model breaks down (denominator = 0 or negative)
P⊂0; = D⊂1; / (r − g) D⊂1; = D⊂0; × (1 + g) r = D⊂1;/P⊂0; + g (required return)
  • P/E (trailing): price / last 12mo EPS; forward P/E: price / next 12mo EPS estimate
  • Justified P/E = (1 − b) / (r − g) where b = retention ratio
  • P/B: price / book value per share; useful for financial firms
  • P/S: price / sales per share; useful when EPS negative
  • P/CF (EV/EBITDA): removes capital structure and D&A effects
  • PEG ratio: P/E divided by earnings growth rate; <1 may indicate undervaluation
Justified P/B = ROE / r − g / (r − g) EV = Mkt Cap + Debt − Cash
  • Asset-based valuation: value = fair value of assets − fair value of liabilities
  • Best for: companies with substantial tangible assets (real estate, holding companies, liquidation scenarios)
  • Book value often understates asset values (historical cost basis)
  • Analyst must adjust for: hidden assets, off-BS liabilities, intangible values
  • Often used as floor valuation (minimum value)
Intrinsic Value = FV Assets − FV Liabilities
  • Intrinsic value > market price → undervalued; buy signal
  • Multiple approaches: use DDM, multiples, and asset-based to triangulate
